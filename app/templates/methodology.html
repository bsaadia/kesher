What this data is, and isn't

Every marker, timeline, and chart on this site represents a message the IDF chose to publish on its official Telegram channel, not an independent record of events on the ground. The IDF decides what to announce, when to announce it, and how to phrase it. That means the same real-world event can be over-represented (multiple messages about one strike), under-represented (no announcement at all), or framed in ways that shape how it reads. This app maps messaging activity. It should be read as a lens on communication strategy and operational tempo, not as a casualty count or ground-truth record of the war.

Collection

Messages are scraped directly from the IDF's official Telegram channel using an authenticated Telegram client. As of this writing, the underlying dataset includes roughly 17,000 raw scraped messages, of which about 3,200 have been geographically tagged for analysis.

Geotagging

Locations are assigned using a gazetteer of about 208 named places across the six fronts, with Hebrew, Arabic, and English name variants. Hebrew text is normalized by stripping common prefixes (ה, ו, ב, כ, ל, ש) before matching against the gazetteer. This is a deterministic, rule-based approach rather than a per-message AI classification: it's slower to expand but fully reproducible, and any two people running it on the same raw data will get the same tags. Where a message could plausibly reference more than one location, the system errs toward tagging it to all plausible locations rather than guessing a single "best" one, since over-tagging is a smaller distortion than silently dropping a real mention.

Reading the ratios

The messages-per-location heatmap divides the number of messages for a front in a given period by the number of distinct locations mentioned for that front in the same period. A high ratio means coverage was concentrated: relatively few places were mentioned repeatedly. A low ratio, closer to one, means coverage was spread across many distinct places instead. The thin vertical line drawn over each cell encodes the raw message count, so a high ratio driven by heavy volume can be told apart from one produced by very few locations with only a handful of messages each.

Front ordering

Fronts are always listed in the same order: by total distinct message count over the full history, from most to least mentioned, not alphabetically. That order is fixed once from the complete, unfiltered dataset and reused everywhere (the legend, the bar charts, the heatmap rows, and the facet grid), so a front's rank stays stable as you change the date range or granularity instead of reshuffling around whatever subset of data happens to be visible.

What's next

Future versions plan to break messaging down by type (air, ground, projectile, casualties and hostages) once the methodology for that classification is solid enough to publish with confidence.
